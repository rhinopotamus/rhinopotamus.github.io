In our case, \(f_{y'}\) -- or, in Leibniz notation, \(\dfrac{df}{dy'}\) -- is really just a function of \(y\) and \(y'\text{,}\) so it can play the role of \(g\) in the previous step. To help you understand what the book says in the next paragraph, use your result to write down an expression for \(\dfrac{d}{dx} \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y'}\text{.}\)
