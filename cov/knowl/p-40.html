The problem of the brachistochrone (βραχιστος (brachistos) = shortest, χρονος (chronos) = time) is that of determining a path down which a particle will fall from one given point to another in the shortest time. Let the \(y\)-axis for convenience be taken vertically downward, as in Figure 1.5.1, the two fixed points being 1 and 2. The initial velocity \(v_1\) at the point 1 is supposed to be given. In Chapter III we shall see that for a curve defined by an equation of the form \(y=y(x)\) the time of descent from 1 to 2 is \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2g}}\) times the value of the integral

\begin{equation*} I=\int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} \sqrt{\frac{1+(y')^2}{y-\alpha}}\,dx\, , \end{equation*}

where \(g\) is the gravitational constant and \(\alpha\) has the constant value \(\alpha = y_1-\frac{v_1^2}{2g}\text{.}\) The problem of the brachistochrone is then to find among the curves \(y=y(x)\) which pass through two points 1 and 2 one which minimizes the integral \(I\text{.}\)
